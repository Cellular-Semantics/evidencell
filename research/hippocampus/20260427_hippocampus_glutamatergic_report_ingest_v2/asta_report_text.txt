Glutamatergic Neurons in the Hippocampal Formation: Cell Types, Specialized Populations and Regional Properties

Major Glutamatergic Cell Types in Hippocampal Subfields

The hippocampus contains distinct glutamatergic neuron populations across its subfields, including granule cells and mossy cells in the dentate gyrus, pyramidal cells in CA3, CA2, and CA1 regions, and specialized populations in the subiculum and entorhinal cortex. CA2 pyramidal neurons represent a unique population with distinct morphological, electrophysiological, and connectivity properties that differentiate them from neighboring CA1 and CA3 regions.

The hippocampus contains five major populations of excitatory glutamatergic neurons distributed across distinct anatomical subfields (Cembrowski et al., 2016). These include granule cells and mossy cells in the dentate gyrus (DG), and pyramidal cells in the CA3, CA2, and CA1 regions (Cembrowski et al., 2016)(Dale et al., 2015). The entorhinal cortex represents the starting point through which information enters the hippocampus, then reaching the granular cells of the dentate gyrus (Colciago et al., 2016). Neurons of the dentate gyrus send axons (mossy fibers) to the CA3 pyramidal neurons, which are connected to CA1 neurons (Colciago et al., 2016).

CA2 as a Distinct Glutamatergic Population

CA2 area is a small region located between CA1 and CA3 neurons that receives input from the dentate gyrus and from the entorhinal cortex and projects to CA1 pyramidal neurons (Colciago et al., 2016). CA2 pyramidal neurons are endowed with many unique physiological, molecular, and genetic characteristics that make them ideal candidates for assessing regional differences in molecular signals (Caruana et al., 2012). CA2 excitatory cells have relatively large somatic sizes that overall are smaller than distal CA3 cells but larger than proximal CA1 cells (Antonio et al., 2014). The somata of neurons in CA2 are typically larger than those found in either CA3 or CA1, and pyramidal cells in CA2 fall into one of several distinct categories based on the unique branching patterns of their apical dendrites (Caruana et al., 2012).

CA2 cells recorded from the age group of 11-21-day-old rats and mice are reported to have larger leak currents and more negative resting membrane potentials than CA1 neurons, and stronger current injection is required to evoke their action potentials (Antonio et al., 2014). Direct input from entorhinal cortex produces a 5- to 6-fold larger EPSP at the soma of CA2 compared with CA1 pyramidal neurons, which is sufficient to drive action potential output from CA2 but not CA1 (Srinivas et al., 2017). This difference results from more efficient dendritic propagation in CA2 than CA1 due to differences in dendritic morphology and dendritic expression of the hyperpolarization-activated cation current (Ih) (Srinivas et al., 2017). CA2 neurons are enriched for genes encoding mitochondrial proteins compared to CA1 neurons, suggesting a difference in metabolic demand across hippocampal circuits (Alsalman et al., 2025).

Entorhinal Cortex Glutamatergic Populations

The entorhinal cortex contains specialized glutamatergic pyramidal neurons and is part of the medial temporal lobe, lying between the transentorhinal area and hippocampal formation (Strell et al., 2023). The entorhinal cortex acts as the main interface between the hippocampus and neocortex and is divided into two subdivisions-lateral and medial-that exhibit distinct anatomical features and input-output connectivity (Park et al., 2018). Principal neurons in entorhinal cortex layer II are of two types, stellate-like neurons and pyramidal neurons, the former of which express reelin, whereas the latter include a large population of calbindin-expressing neurons (Ohara et al., 2021)(Varga et al., 2010)(Fuchs et al., 2016)(Ohara et al., 2019)(Zutshi et al., 2018). Principal neurons in entorhinal cortex layer III express Purkinje cell protein 4 (PCP4) and project to CA1 and the subiculum (Ohara et al., 2021).

Classical Hippocampal Circuit Organization

Glutamatergic neurons dominate the hippocampal architecture, accounting for over 90% of all hippocampal neurons, with pyramidal layers being densely packed with these excitatory cells (Mancini et al., 2022). These principal cells are interconnected through glutamatergic synapses that form the classical trisynaptic pathway, where dentate granule cells receive input from entorhinal cortex and project to CA3 pyramidal cells, which then connect to CA1 pyramidal cells (Munster-Wandowski et al., 2013)(Yau et al., 2015). The glutamatergic principal cells have excitatory effects on all the neurons they contact, including other glutamatergic neurons as well as GABAergic and monoaminergic cells (Dale et al., 2015). For CA2, we identified some pyramidal cells at the CA3c region while others distributed along the intermediate (CA3b) and distal (CA3a) subregions (Sanchez-Aguilera et al., 2021).

Subicular Glutamatergic Neurons

The subiculum contains glutamatergic pyramidal neurons that serve as a major output structure of the hippocampal formation, transmitting information from CA1 to the entorhinal cortex. These neurons display distinct electrophysiological subtypes with specific spatial distributions and morphological characteristics.

The subiculum functions as one of the major output structures of the hippocampal formation alongside CA1, playing an integral role in hippocampal-cortical information processing (MacDougall et al., 2013). CA1 pyramidal cells project through a topographically organized projection to the subiculum, and the majority of subicular cells conserve their topographic input along the transverse axis from CA1 (MacDougall et al., 2013). These subicular glutamatergic pyramidal neurons then transmit information to the deep layers (layers V and VI) of entorhinal cortex in a spatially structured manner (MacDougall et al., 2013)(Guinet et al., 2026).

Despite their functional importance, subicular glutamatergic neurons remain relatively understudied compared to other hippocampal regions, with only 6 of the 180 Hippocampome.org neuron types being from the subiculum, compared to more than 60 in the adjacent area CA1 (Tecuatl et al., 2025). However, research has revealed high electrophysiological and molecular heterogeneity among subicular pyramidal neurons, with evidence for further spatial subdivisions within the subiculum (Guinet et al., 2026).

Subicular pyramidal neurons can be classified into three distinct electrophysiological subtypes based on their discharge patterns: regular firing (RF), weak-bursting (WB), and strong-bursting (SB) neurons (Guinet et al., 2026). These three subtypes show differences in their active and passive electrophysiological properties, as well as distinct morphometric features including variations in apical and basal dendritic spread and branching patterns (Guinet et al., 2026). Among RF neurons, there exists a divergent morphological subset that bears two apical dendrites, further adding to the structural diversity of subicular glutamatergic populations (Guinet et al., 2026). These subtypes also display specific spatial distributions along both the superficial-deep and proximo-distal axes of the subiculum (Guinet et al., 2026).

The subiculum also provides feedback connections to earlier stages of the hippocampal circuit, with anatomical and electrophysiological evidence showing that subicular neurons provide excitatory synaptic input back to CA1 pyramidal cells (Xu et al., 2016). This bidirectional connectivity allows the subiculum to modulate hippocampal processing while serving its primary role as an output structure.

Mossy Cells: Specialized Glutamatergic Neurons

Mossy cells are large glutamatergic neurons located in the dentate hilus that are distinguished by their characteristic thorny excrescences and complex axonal projections. They represent the second type of excitatory principal cell in the dentate gyrus alongside granule cells, making the dentate gyrus unique among hippocampal subfields.

The dentate gyrus is unique among hippocampal subfields because it contains two distinct types of glutamatergic principal cells: the numerous granule cells and the specialized mossy cells (Scharfman et al., 2013). Mossy cells are large glutamatergic neurons located in the dentate hilus and represent a major cell type that innervates both granule cells and local GABAergic interneurons (Scharfman et al., 2015)(Botterill et al., 2021). These cells are the principal and only glutamatergic neurons in the dentate hilus (Sun et al., 2017).

The most distinctive morphological feature of mossy cells is their characteristic thorny excrescences - complex spines that encrust their proximal dendrites and give them a "mossy" appearance (Botterill et al., 2021)(Fredes et al., 2021)(Sun et al., 2017). These thorny excrescences receive excitatory synaptic input from mossy fiber terminals of granule cells (Fredes et al., 2021). Mossy cells also have relatively large cell bodies and thick, bushy proximal dendrites that remain mainly within the hilus (Fredes et al., 2021)(Botterill et al., 2021).

The glutamatergic nature of mossy cells has been confirmed through both anatomical and physiological evidence. Anatomical studies demonstrated glutamate immunoreactivity in Golgi-impregnated mossy cells, while physiological recordings showed that mossy cells produce excitatory postsynaptic potentials in granule cells, confirming their excitatory glutamatergic function (Scharfman et al., 2013)(Fredes et al., 2021).

Mossy cells have complex axonal projection patterns that distinguish them from other hippocampal glutamatergic neurons. Their axons make local collaterals near the cell body that terminate in the hilus, but the majority of their axonal projections extend distally to terminate in the inner molecular layer of the dentate gyrus across multiple septotemporal levels (Botterill et al., 2021)(Fredes et al., 2021). Additionally, mossy cells have commissural projections that terminate in the contralateral dentate gyrus (Botterill et al., 2021). This extensive axonal distribution allows mossy cells to influence dentate gyrus function across broad spatial domains.

Electrophysiological Properties and Firing Patterns

Glutamatergic neurons in the hippocampus show diverse electrophysiological properties and firing patterns, including fast-firing, cluster-firing, burst-firing, and slow-firing types. Some populations can fire rhythmically at theta frequencies and display unique characteristics like cluster firing with subthreshold membrane oscillations.

Glutamatergic neurons in the hippocampus display highly heterogeneous electrophysiological properties and firing patterns (Huh et al., 2010)(Senova et al., 2020). These neurons can be classified into several distinct groups based on their firing characteristics, including fast-firing, cluster-firing, burst-firing, and slow-firing types (Huh et al., 2010)(Muller et al., 2017).

The largest group consists of fast-spiking neurons that show little action potential accommodation and sometimes display spontaneous firing (Muller et al., 2017). Some of these fast-firing glutamatergic neurons show a pronounced sag in response to hyperpolarizing current injection, similar to properties observed in GABAergic neurons (Muller et al., 2017)(Huh et al., 2010).

A particularly distinctive firing pattern unique to glutamatergic neurons is cluster firing, characterized by clusters of action potentials interspersed with subthreshold membrane oscillations (Huh et al., 2010)(Muller et al., 2017). This cluster firing pattern occurs at frequencies of 8-14 Hz and is found in approximately half of glutamatergic neurons (Senova et al., 2020). These neurons also show strong action potential accommodation and little or no sag response (Muller et al., 2017).

Glutamatergic neurons also exhibit burst-firing patterns that are distinct from burst firing observed in GABAergic cells (Huh et al., 2010)(Muller et al., 2017). Additionally, some glutamatergic neurons display slow-firing patterns with broad action potentials and accommodating properties, similar to cholinergic neurons (Huh et al., 2010)(Muller et al., 2017).

Notably, many glutamatergic neurons can fire rhythmically at theta frequencies and show prominent Ih currents (Huh et al., 2010)(Senova et al., 2020). These intrinsic firing properties may play important roles in pacing hippocampal activity and generating theta oscillations (Senova et al., 2020). Early-generated glutamatergic neurons display distinct morpho-functional features that contribute to the functional diversity within the apparently homogeneous glutamatergic population (Marissal et al., 2012).

Synaptic Properties and Neurotransmitter Systems

Glutamatergic neurons in the hippocampus use complex synaptic machinery including AMPA, NMDA, and metabotropic glutamate receptors, with some populations capable of co-releasing both glutamate and GABA. These cells express specific vesicular transporters and show distinct receptor distributions across hippocampal subfields.

Hippocampal glutamatergic neurons utilize sophisticated synaptic machinery centered around ionotropic and metabotropic glutamate receptors. Fast excitatory synaptic transmission in principal projection neurons is primarily mediated by AMPA receptors, while NMDA receptors serve a slower modulatory role (Kirson et al., 2000). Virtually all projection neurons across hippocampal subfields contain subunits from the AMPA/kainate, kainate, and NMDA receptor families, with these receptors being broadly colocalized in hippocampal neurons and even at individual dendritic spines (Siegel et al., 1995).

The main targets of glutamatergic input are dendritic spines of pyramidal cells and granule cells, as well as dendritic shafts of interneurons (Martin-Belmonte et al., 2020). AMPA receptors consist of tetrameric complexes composed of combinations of GluA1-4 subunits, with the majority of hippocampal AMPARs consisting of heteromeric combinations of GluA1, GluA2, and other subunits (Martin-Belmonte et al., 2020). These receptor subunits show distinct distribution patterns across hippocampal regions, with GluA1 displaying diffuse staining in CA3 and strong immunoreactivity in CA1 stratum oriens and radiatum, while GluA2 shows uniform staining with greater localization to pyramidal cell bodies (Yeung et al., 2020).

Metabotropic glutamate receptors also play important roles, with mGluR1 mainly expressed in granule cells and CA3 pyramidal neurons, while mGluR5 is highly expressed in all hippocampal subfields (Sarvari et al., 2016). The vesicular glutamate transporter vGLUT1 is the main subtype expressed in the hippocampus, packing glutamate into synaptic vesicles of glutamatergic axon terminals (Sarvari et al., 2016).

Remarkably, some glutamatergic neurons can co-release both glutamate and GABA. Granule cells, though primarily glutamatergic, contain GABA, its synthesizing enzyme, and the vesicular GABA transporter in their mossy fiber axons (Zander et al., 2010). This dual neurotransmitter capability allows for mixed GABAergic and glutamatergic synaptic currents in CA3 principal cells . Additionally, glutamatergic neurons can form mixed synapses that combine chemical glutamatergic transmission with electrical coupling through gap junctions, as observed in CA3 pyramidal neurons (Hamzei-Sichani et al., 2012).

Developmental and Temporal Characteristics

Glutamatergic neurons in the hippocampus show distinct developmental timelines, with granule cells having delayed postnatal maturation and early-generated glutamatergic populations displaying unique morpho-functional features. The timing of neurogenesis creates functional diversity within apparently homogeneous glutamatergic populations.

Hippocampal glutamatergic neurons display remarkable developmental heterogeneity that contributes to their functional diversity. Granule cells are characterized by their peculiar delayed and heterogeneous maturation, with most of them (85%) being generated postnatally (Pedroni et al., 2014). This extended developmental timeline allows granule cells to integrate into existing hippocampal circuits in a temporally regulated manner.

The maturation of glutamatergic synaptic properties follows a specific developmental sequence. New granule cells receive their first glutamatergic input on dendritic spines at the end of the second week after birth, and then project their first glutamatergic output onto area CA3 (Hagihara et al., 2011). The expression of glutamate receptors also shows temporal regulation, with granule cells beginning to profoundly express GluR1 at around 3 weeks after cell division (Hagihara et al., 2011).

Young granule cells receive remarkably diverse glutamatergic inputs during development. Very young, one-week-old new granule cells receive input not only from GABAergic interneurons, but also from multiple glutamatergic cell types, including mature dentate granule cells, area CA1-3 pyramidal cells and mossy cells (Sah et al., 2017). This broad connectivity pattern suggests that developing glutamatergic neurons are rapidly integrated into complex hippocampal networks.

The timing of neurogenesis creates lasting functional diversity within glutamatergic populations. Early-generated glutamatergic neurons display distinct morpho-functional features that persist from the juvenile through the adult hippocampus (Marissal et al., 2012). This sub-population of early-generated glutamatergic neurons impacts network dynamics when stimulated in the juvenile hippocampus, demonstrating that the apparently homogeneous glutamatergic cell population displays morpho-functional diversity rooted in temporal embryonic origins (Marissal et al., 2012).

Specialized Glutamatergic Populations

Beyond the major hippocampal glutamatergic neurons, the hippocampus contains several specialized glutamatergic populations including semilunar granule cells in the dentate gyrus, Cajal-Retzius cells, calretinin-positive neurons, calbindin-positive cells, dopamine receptor-expressing pyramidal neurons, and unique glial-like cells. These populations have distinct molecular markers, connectivity patterns, and functional roles in hippocampal circuits.

Semilunar granule cells (SGCs): These are sparse dentate gyrus glutamatergic projection neurons with unique morphological and physiological properties that distinguish them from regular granule cells (Dovek et al., 2025)(Williams et al., 2007). SGCs have significantly larger dendritic arborization in the molecular layer, more triangular cell bodies, and axon collaterals in the molecular layer (Williams et al., 2007). Unlike regular granule cells, SGCs fire throughout long-duration depolarizing steps and have ramp-like depolarizations during interspike periods (Williams et al., 2007). SGCs receive stronger medial entorhinal cortex and associational synaptic drive but lack the short-term facilitation of lateral entorhinal cortex inputs observed in granule cells (Dovek et al., 2025). These neurons monosynaptically excite both hilar interneurons and mossy cells and show overrepresentation among neuronal ensembles in spatial memory tasks (Williams et al., 2007)(Dovek et al., 2025).

Cajal-Retzius cells: These are glutamatergic neurons found in stratum lacunosum-moleculare that are more abundant in adult rats than previously assumed (Wheeler et al., 2015)(Quattrocolo et al., 2014). Cajal-Retzius cells can be both GABAergic and glutamatergic, with glutamatergic Cajal-Retzius cells releasing glutamate to facilitate migration of GABAergic neurons and interneurons (Yu et al., 2014)(Manent et al., 2006). These cells form direct monosynaptic connections with interneurons and activate both AMPA and NMDA receptors (Quattrocolo et al., 2014).

Calretinin-positive (CR) glutamatergic neurons: These specialized cells can be subdivided into distinct populations based on their local versus projecting axonal characteristics (Anstotz et al., 2015). Some CR cells have complete layer-restricted location of both somatodendritic and axonal domains, functioning as "glutamatergic interneurons," while others have long-range axons that project to distant hippocampal regions. Notably, CR cells provide the first evidence of intrinsic glutamatergic hippocampal connections that flow against the classical trisynaptic circuit direction (Anstotz et al., 2015)(Ceranik et al., 1997).

Calbindin-positive glutamatergic neurons: In the entorhinal cortex, 88% of calbindin-positive cells are glutamatergic and form discrete cell clusters (Naumann et al., 2015). These calbindin-positive pyramidal cells show strong theta modulation and are arranged in a hexagonal grid of patches that receives cholinergic innervation (Naumann et al., 2015).

Neuroplastin-65 positive glutamatergic neurons: These include granule neurons of the dentate gyrus, pyramidal neurons of CA1, CA2-3, subiculum, and specific layers of entorhinal cortex (Herrera-Molina et al., 2017)(Langnaese et al., 1997). Neuroplastin-65 is abundant at membranes of cell bodies, dendrites, and in punctate structures within the neuropil, and plays important roles in regulating excitatory synapse number and function (Herrera-Molina et al., 2017)(Herrera-Molina et al., 2014).

Dopamine receptor-expressing pyramidal neurons: In the ventral hippocampus, glutamatergic neurons represent 45.1% of all D1 or D2 dopamine receptor-positive cells, in contrast to dorsal hippocampus where these receptors are almost exclusively found on interneurons (Godino et al., 2023)(Puighermanal et al., 2016). These glutamatergic pyramidal neuron clusters likely correspond to projection-specific ventral CA1/ventral subiculum populations (Godino et al., 2023).

GluR cells: These are unique glutamate receptor-expressing cells that escape classification as neurons, astrocytes, or oligodendrocytes (Jabs et al., 2005). GluR cells co-express S100β (astrocyte marker), NG2, and neuronal genes, and receive direct synaptic input from both GABAergic and glutamatergic neurons. These cells show characteristic nodules along their processes and represent a distinct population that bridges glial and neuronal properties (Jabs et al., 2005).
